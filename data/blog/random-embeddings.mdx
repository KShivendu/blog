> Looking for TL;DR? Skip to [key takeaways](#key-takeaways)

Generate 8,000 random 1024-dimensional unit vectors and ask a simple question: which vector is any given vector's nearest neighbor, and how near is it? The answer barely means anything. The closest of all 8,000 sits at cosine **0.12**; a random pair sits at **0.00**. The "nearest neighbor" is almost exactly as far away as everything else.

Do the same with real embeddings — [`mxbai-embed-large-v1`](https://huggingface.co/mixedbread-ai/mxbai-embed-large-v1) over 8,000 short text strings, same count, same 1024 dimensions — and the picture is completely different: the nearest neighbor sits at cosine **0.73**, the median pair at **0.39**. There's a real gap between "near" and "typical."

That gap is the entire game. Nearest-neighbor search lives or dies on it. This post is about what random high-dimensional vectors actually look like, why search can't do anything with them, and what "structure" means as the cure — measured by what it does to search compute and recall.

## Random vectors have no neighbors

In high dimensions, random points concentrate: nearly all pairwise distances pile up around the same value. This is the [concentration of distances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curse_of_dimensionality#Nearest_neighbor_search), and it's been known to break nearest-neighbor search since [Beyer et al. (1999)](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/3-540-49257-7_15) asked "when is nearest neighbor meaningful?". For random unit vectors the answer is: essentially never. Two random directions in 1024-d are almost always orthogonal (cosine ≈ 0), and even the closest of thousands is only nudged off orthogonal.

For real embeddings the bars fall off a cliff: the nearest is much closer than typical, and there's a clear ordering among the top neighbors. For random vectors the bars are flat and low — the nearest neighbor (0.12) is barely distinguishable from a coin-flip pair (0.00). **There is no neighborhood to find.**

Why is the random "nearest neighbor" so far away? Because in high dimensions, almost every pair of vectors is **perpendicular**.

## In high dimensions, everything is perpendicular

Pick two random directions in `d` dimensions and measure the cosine between them. In 2D or 3D they're often quite aligned. As `d` grows, the cosine concentrates hard around 0 — two random unit vectors are almost certainly nearly orthogonal. Drag the dimension below and hit resample; watch the distribution of pairwise cosines collapse onto 0:

<HighDimOrthogonality />

The width of that distribution shrinks as exactly **1/√d**. The intuition: a unit vector spreads its length across `d` coordinates, so each is about `1/√d` in size; the dot product of two independent unit vectors sums `d` such terms with random signs, landing near 0 with standard deviation `1/√d`. Measured against theory, it's a near-perfect match:

The consequence is brutal for search. By 1024 dimensions, **99.9% of random pairs sit within ±0.1 cosine of perfectly orthogonal** — up from 6.5% in 2D:

When everything is perpendicular to everything else, "nearest" stops meaning anything: no direction is meaningfully closer than any other. That's the geometry real embeddings have to escape — and the rest of this post is about how they do it.

## What that costs a search index

[HNSW](https://arxiv.org/abs/1603.09320) finds nearest neighbors by greedily walking a graph: from some entry node, hop to whichever neighbor is closer to the query, repeat. The walk only works if there's a distance _gradient_ to follow — each step has to get meaningfully closer. Random vectors have no gradient (every direction is ~equidistant), so the walk wanders and the index has to compensate by exploring far more candidates.

I built HNSW indexes (M=16, efConstruction=200) over both datasets and swept the search-time effort knob `efSearch`, measuring recall@10 against exact brute force and the query latency. Mechanism first, then the numbers: real data should converge quickly and cheaply; random data should need a much wider search for less reward.

Real embeddings hit **0.90 recall by efSearch=32**, at **0.016ms** per query, and stay pinned there. Random embeddings never reach it: even at efSearch=256 — **8× the search effort and 5× the latency** — they top out at **0.855**, and the gap only closes slowly because the search is fighting the geometry. Note that this is the _same index algorithm, the same 1024 dimensions, the same 8,000 vectors_. The only thing that changed is whether the vectors have structure.

## So what is "structure"?

"Structure" is vague, so let me make it operational: I started from pure random vectors and injected structure three ways, then watched what each does to search. Each is a knob from "random" toward "real."

1. **Low intrinsic dimension** — generate the vectors inside an `r`-dimensional subspace, then embed that into 1024-d. Sweep `r` from 2 to 1024.
2. **Spectral decay** — keep all 1024 dimensions but scale them by a decaying spectrum (`i^(-α/2)`), so a few directions dominate. Sweep `α`. This is the [anisotropy](/blog/embedding-anisotropy) real embeddings are famous for.
3. **Clusters** — draw the vectors from a mixture of 64 centers and sweep how tight the clusters are.

To put all three knobs on a common axis I use **intrinsic dimension** — roughly, how many numbers you'd actually need to pin down a point, regardless of how many it's stored with. A sheet of paper crumpled inside a 3D room is still intrinsically 2D; random vectors are the opposite, using up nearly every dimension they're given. I estimate it with the [TwoNN](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-017-11873-y) method (random data comes out at ~230, real mxbai at ~**14**). Plotting query latency against intrinsic dimension (each point is one setting; lower-left is cheaper) separates two distinct mechanisms:

**Two mechanisms, and they're not the same:**

- **Lowering effective dimensionality** (subspace rank _or_ spectral decay) helps, and the gain _tracks intrinsic dimension_ — both curves slide down-and-left together. This is the direct cure for distance concentration: collapse onto a low-dimensional surface and neighbors become meaningful again.
- **Clustering helps through a different door.** As clusters tighten, latency drops **4–5×** (0.056 → 0.013ms) while the intrinsic dimension stays _high_ (~145). The cluster points fall almost straight down, off the dimensionality trend. Clusters don't reduce dimensionality — they create separable basins, so the greedy walk routes between blobs cheaply even though each blob is still high-dimensional. The intrinsic-dimension estimate can't even see this win.

Real mxbai (the red star) has both: a low intrinsic dimension (~14) _and_ cluster structure — which is why it sits where it does, far cheaper than random.

There's a second, quieter result hiding in this sweep. **Structure mostly buys compute, not recall.** Once _any_ of these knobs is turned even slightly past "random," recall snaps to the ~0.90 HNSW ceiling and stays there; the dramatic 4–5× swings are all in latency. The recall gap is really just the one-time jump from random's 0.81 floor to the structured 0.90 ceiling — after that, structure is a _compute_ story, not an accuracy one.

## How do you measure structure?

I've been waving at "structure" — low intrinsic dimension, clusters, anisotropy. Can we put a single number on it? Four candidate measures, computed on every dataset (latency from the sweep above, for reference):

<div style={{overflowX: 'auto'}}>

| dataset         | anisotropy | IsoScore | participation ratio | intrinsic dim |  HNSW latency |
| --------------- | ---------: | -------: | ------------------: | ------------: | ------------: |
| random          |      +0.00 | **0.89** |                 908 |           230 |     0.0578 ms |
| intrinsic r=8   |       0.00 |     0.01 |                   8 |             7 |     0.0242 ms |
| intrinsic r=128 |       0.00 |     0.11 |                 113 |            65 |     0.0537 ms |
| spectral α=0.5  |       0.00 |     0.48 |                 494 |           177 |     0.0546 ms |
| spectral α=3    |       0.00 |     0.00 |                   2 |            12 |     0.0172 ms |
| clusters c=0.5  |      +0.00 |     0.57 |                 582 |           154 |     0.0339 ms |
| clusters c=8    |      +0.02 |     0.06 |                  61 |           140 | **0.0128 ms** |
| real (mxbai)    |  **+0.39** |     0.13 |                 132 |            14 |     0.0372 ms |

</div>

- **Anisotropy** — the average cosine between random pairs ([Mu & Viswanath](https://arxiv.org/abs/1702.01417), [Ethayarajh](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.00512)). 0 means random pairs are orthogonal on average.
- **IsoScore** ([Rudman et al. 2022](https://aclanthology.org/2022.findings-acl.262/)) — how uniformly variance spreads across dimensions, PCA-reoriented and mean/scale-invariant. 1 = fully isotropic, 0 = one direction dominates.
- **Participation ratio** — `(Σλ)² / Σλ²` over the covariance eigenvalues: the linear "effective dimension."
- **Intrinsic dimension** (TwoNN) — the nonlinear, local version from above.

Two things jump out.

**Average-cosine anisotropy is almost useless here.** It's ~0 for random _and_ for every synthetic-structured dataset — even the r=8 subspace, which is as structured as data gets. It only fires for real mxbai (+0.39), and what it detects there is narrow and specific: real embeddings sit in an [off-center cone](/blog/embedding-anisotropy) (a shared "common direction"), which is a different thing from low dimensionality or clustering. This is exactly the [critique that motivated IsoScore](https://aclanthology.org/2022.findings-acl.262/) — a point cloud can be intensely structured and still look perfectly "isotropic" by average cosine.

**For nearest-neighbor search, isotropy is the enemy.** Note the twist: random vectors are the _most_ isotropic thing in the table (IsoScore 0.89 — they fill every dimension equally) and the _worst_ to search. Every structured dataset has _lower_ IsoScore. So the usual NLP framing — "isotropy good, anisotropy bad" — is about a different problem (cosine saturation). For search you _want_ anisotropy: variance piled into fewer directions is exactly what gives the greedy walk a gradient to follow.

Which single number best predicts whether search is cheap? Honestly, none of them nails it — and the reason is the two mechanisms from the last section. IsoScore, participation ratio, and intrinsic dimension all measure _how many dimensions the data really uses_, so they track the low-effective-dimension story well; but none cleanly captures _clustering_. Look at `clusters c=8`: intrinsic dimension stays high (~140, every cluster is locally high-dimensional) yet it's the cheapest of all to search. There's no one scalar for "structure" — but if you only get one, intrinsic dimension or IsoScore both beat the average-cosine anisotropy the field leaned on for years.

## You can't add structure in the index

If structure is what makes search cheap, the obvious next question is whether you can bolt it on _after_ the embeddings are fixed — at index-build time, keeping the vectors (and therefore the true neighbors, and recall) untouched. I tried the three natural levers. All three are negatives, and the negatives are the point.

**Insertion order.** HNSW is built incrementally, so the order you insert vectors changes the graph. I tried inserting cluster-by-cluster, cluster-medoids-first, and densest-points-first against a random baseline. The graph changes; the cost doesn't.

<div style={{overflowX: 'auto'}}>

| insert order (real embeddings) | recall@10 | distance comps / query |
| ------------------------------ | --------: | ---------------------: |
| random                         |     0.900 |                  1,022 |
| cluster-blocked                |     0.900 |                  1,027 |
| medoid-first                   |     0.900 |                  1,024 |
| hub-first                      |     0.900 |                  1,024 |

</div>

**Smart entry points.** HNSW spends hops descending its upper layers to find a good place to start the base-layer search. So I tried skipping that: route the query to its cluster first (compare to 64 centroids) and start the search right inside that cluster. It didn't help — and the reason is the useful part. A single _fixed_ entry point with **no hierarchy at all** costs the same as the full hierarchy:

<div style={{overflowX: 'auto'}}>

| entry strategy (real embeddings) | recall@10 | distance comps / query |
| -------------------------------- | --------: | ---------------------: |
| full HNSW hierarchy              |     0.900 |                  1,016 |
| single global fixed entry        |     0.900 |              **1,001** |
| cluster-routed entry             |     0.900 |                  1,016 |

</div>

At a practical `efSearch`, the cost is dominated by the **base-layer beam** — the work of examining the ~`efSearch` candidates around the query to polish the answer — not by _getting_ to the right neighborhood. A better starting point only saves the cheap descent. (This flips at very large N, where the descent grows ~log N and routing starts to matter — worth revisiting at 100M+ scale, but at 8k it's noise.)

The third lever, `M` and `efConstruction`, _does_ move the numbers — but only by trading compute for recall along the usual curve, not by manufacturing a free speedup.

The throughline: none of the index-side tricks make search cheaper for free. The ~3× spread we keep seeing — random at ~1,875 distance comps vs real at ~1,020 vs tight clusters at ~570 — comes from the data's own geometry, which is baked in when the embeddings are produced. An index can trade compute for recall; it can't put structure into vectors that don't have it.

<details>
<summary>**Aside: HNSW indexes are nondeterministic** — and it doesn't matter</summary>

Rebuild the same HNSW index over the same vectors and you get a _different graph every time_ — not because of a random seed or insertion order, but because the default multi-threaded build has a race in how concurrent neighbor updates land. With the same seed and same order, the saved index differs byte-for-byte on every run; only single-threaded construction (`num_threads=1`) reproduces it exactly.

The reassuring part: across all those different graphs, recall and latency are stable to within ~0.1%. Different graph, same quality. So the nondeterminism is real but benign — worth knowing if you ever try to diff or cache indexes by hash.

</details>

## Key Takeaways

- **High-dimensional random vectors have no neighbors.** The nearest of 8,000 random 1024-d vectors sits at cosine 0.12 vs 0.00 for a random pair — barely nearer than nothing. Real embeddings show a real gap (0.73 vs 0.39), and that gap is what nearest-neighbor search depends on.
- **In high dimensions, everything is perpendicular.** The cosine between two random vectors concentrates at 0 with spread exactly 1/√d; by 1024-d, 99.9% of random pairs are within ±0.1 of orthogonal. With no direction meaningfully closer, "nearest" loses its meaning.
- **Random embeddings are expensive to search and cap out lower.** Same index, same dimensions: real hits 0.90 recall at efSearch=32; random never beats 0.855 even at 8× the search effort.
- **"Structure" is two things.** Low effective dimensionality (cures distance concentration; gain tracks intrinsic dim) and clustering (separable basins; 4–5× cheaper search _independent_ of dimensionality). Real embeddings have both — intrinsic dimension ~14 plus clusters.
- **Structure mostly buys compute, not recall.** Past the random→structured jump, recall pins at the HNSW ceiling; the big wins are all in latency.
- **For search, isotropy is the enemy.** Random is the most isotropic data and the worst to search. Average-cosine anisotropy barely detects structure (it only flags real's off-center cone); IsoScore and intrinsic dimension capture it far better — though no single scalar fully predicts search cost, because clustering is a separate axis.
- **You can't add structure in the index.** Insertion order and smart entry points give nothing at this scale (the base-layer beam dominates, not the descent); `M`/`efConstruction` only trade compute for recall. The geometry is set when the embeddings are made — the index can't fake it.
