In [Inference-Free SPLADE](/blog/if-splade) I removed the transformer from the _query_ side of sparse search: documents still get neural expansion at index time, but queries are just tokenized. The result was full SPLADE quality at 13× lower latency.

Static embeddings push that idea to its limit on the _dense_ side: there is no transformer at query time **or** index time. A document vector is computed by looking up one vector per token from a fixed table and averaging them. No attention, no forward pass — `O(tokens)` table lookups plus a mean.

This is conceptually word2vec/GloVe, but the lookup tables are **distilled or trained from modern sentence transformers**, so they're far better than classic word vectors. Two families dominate today:

- [**Model2Vec**](https://github.com/MinishLab/model2vec) — distills a sentence transformer into a static table via a forward pass over the vocabulary, then PCA and Zipf re-weighting. [`potion-base-32M`](https://huggingface.co/minishlab/potion-base-32M) is distilled from BGE-base on **C4 web text** (general-purpose); [`potion-retrieval-32M`](https://huggingface.co/minishlab/potion-retrieval-32M) then fine-tunes that on a **retrieval mix (MS MARCO, GooAQ, SQuAD, S2ORC, NQ, TriviaQA, PubMedQA…)**.
- [**`sentence-transformers/static-retrieval-mrl-en-v1`**](https://huggingface.co/sentence-transformers/static-retrieval-mrl-en-v1) — a _trained_ `EmbeddingBag` ([Tom Aarsen's recipe](https://huggingface.co/blog/static-embeddings)) with [Matryoshka](https://huggingface.co/blog/matryoshka) dimensions (1024 → 32), trained on **80M retrieval pairs** from the same QA/MS MARCO family.

The training data matters: it tells you where each model should be strong (QA, paraphrase, low lexical overlap) and where it won't be. I benchmarked them against a full transformer dense baseline and BM25 across **four BEIR datasets**, all on CPU. The speedups are enormous, and the quality story has a clean pattern — static dense beats BM25 exactly where lexical matching breaks down.

## How It Works

<StaticPipeline />

The only difference from a normal sentence transformer is the middle. A transformer runs 6–12 attention layers; a static model replaces that entire stack with a single lookup table. Everything else — tokenize, pool, cosine — is identical.

```python
from model2vec import StaticModel

model = StaticModel.from_pretrained("minishlab/potion-retrieval-32M")
# encode = tokenize → look up one row per token → mean-pool. No transformer.
vecs = model.encode(["heart attack", "cardiac arrest"])
```

Because there's no forward pass, this runs at thousands of documents per second on a CPU. The price you pay is in what a lookup-then-average _can't_ represent — more on that below.

## Results on scifact

I started on [BEIR scifact](https://github.com/beir-cellar/beir) (5,183 docs, 300 test queries, scientific claim verification) — the same dataset as the SPLADE post. Exact brute-force cosine search, everything on CPU. Query latency is per query (batch size 1), the real query path.

Two things jump out. First, **the retrieval-tuned static model is far better than the general one**: `potion-retrieval-32M` (0.6345) beats its own base `potion-base-32M` (0.5618) by **13%**, just from fine-tuning the same table on retrieval data. Always use the retrieval variant.

Second, the headline tradeoff: `potion-retrieval-32M` reaches **97.9% of all-MiniLM's NDCG@10** (0.6345 vs 0.6484) — and actually **beats it on Recall@10** (0.7948 vs 0.7883) — while embedding queries **~90× faster** (0.13ms vs 12.1ms) and indexing **~80× faster** (1,562 vs 19 docs/sec). It lands within 4.5% of BM25, at a query cost indistinguishable from BM25's.

## Where Static Embeddings Actually Win

scifact is one dataset, and a lexically dense one — queries and relevant documents share rare, specific terms (gene names, conditions), which is exactly BM25's strength. To see where static dense pays off, I ran the same benchmark on three more BEIR datasets spanning the lexical-overlap spectrum: [**arguana**](https://huggingface.co/datasets/BeIR/arguana) (argument → counter-argument, almost no shared vocabulary), [**nfcorpus**](https://huggingface.co/datasets/BeIR/nfcorpus) (medical), and [**fiqa**](https://huggingface.co/datasets/BeIR/fiqa) (financial QA). Transformer corpora were encoded on a Modal A10G; static and BM25 stayed on CPU.

| ------------------------ | ---------- | ---------- | ----------- | ------ |
| BM25                     | **0.6645** | **0.3105** | 0.3152      | 0.2318 |
| **potion-retrieval-32M** | 0.6345     | 0.3076     | **0.3327**  | 0.1885 |
| static-retrieval-mrl     | 0.5935     | 0.3004     | **0.3314**  | 0.1996 |
| all-MiniLM-L6-v2         | 0.6484     | 0.3179     | 0.3664      | 0.3637 |
| bge-small-en-v1.5        | 0.7215     | 0.3402     | 0.4305      | 0.3884 |

</div>

The pattern is clean: **a static embedding's win over BM25 tracks how little lexical overlap the task has.**

- **arguana** — queries are arguments, relevant docs are _counter-arguments_ that rarely reuse the query's words. BM25 has little to grip. Here **both static models beat BM25** (potion-retrieval 0.333 vs 0.315, +5.6%) — the clean win, at ~0.1ms/query.
- **nfcorpus** — static essentially **ties** BM25 (0.308 vs 0.311).
- **scifact / fiqa** — lexically dense; BM25 wins. On fiqa, financial jargon overlaps heavily and a real transformer (MiniLM 0.364) is needed to beat BM25 — static (0.20) can't.

So the honest reading: a static embedding buys you **~90× lower query latency**, and on low-lexical-overlap retrieval it **beats BM25 outright**. On lexical-heavy corpora it trails BM25, and if you need to clear BM25 there you need a real transformer (and its latency). Note that the static-vs-BM25 verdict flips on the _task_, not the model — pick static when vocabulary mismatch, not exact-term matching, is your problem.

## The Defining Limitation: Word Order

Here's the mechanism behind the quality gap, and it's a clean one. A static sentence vector is `mean(lookup(t) for t in tokens)`. Mean-pooling is **permutation-invariant**: reorder the tokens and the average is byte-for-byte identical. The model literally cannot tell two reorderings of the same words apart.

Two things worth noting. First, the transformer is only _approximately_ order-sensitive on short sentences — "dog bites man" vs "man bites dog" still scores 0.91, not far from the static 1.000. Mean-pooling over contextualized tokens washes out a lot of word order even in a transformer. Second, and more useful: the bottom row ("surged" vs "plunged") swaps a _word_, not the order — and there the static model (0.80) does about as well as the transformer (0.73).

So the cost of going static is specifically **word order and composition**, not vocabulary. If your relevance signal lives in which words appear — which is most of retrieval — static holds up. If it lives in how they're arranged ("flight NY→London" vs "London→NY", negation, role reversal), static is blind by construction.

## Matryoshka: Quality vs Dimension

`static-retrieval-mrl-en-v1` is trained with [Matryoshka representation learning](https://huggingface.co/blog/matryoshka), so you can truncate the 1024-d vector to fewer dimensions and just re-normalize — no re-encoding. Since static models have no depth (no layers), this dimension axis is the closest thing they have to a quality knob. Here's how NDCG@10 degrades as you shrink it:

Truncating to **256-d keeps 95% of full quality at a quarter of the storage** (0.564 vs 0.594). The curve stays gentle down to 128-d, then falls off a cliff: 64-d drops to 77% of full, and 32-d to just 52%. The sweet spot for this model is 256–512 dimensions — small enough to be cheap, large enough to dodge the cliff.

## Where Static Embeddings Fit

Static embeddings are to dense retrieval what BM25 is to sparse: the **no-model baseline**. The whole spectrum, from the SPLADE post and this one:

- **Sparse:** BM25 (no model) → inference-free SPLADE (model at index only) → full SPLADE (model both sides)
- **Dense:** static embeddings (tiny lookup, no transformer) → … → full transformer dense

Reach for static when: latency or CPU/GPU budget is the binding constraint, the corpus is large and re-encoding cost matters, you need an embedding you can run anywhere (edge, mobile, a Lambda with no GPU), **or your task has low lexical overlap** (paraphrase, argument, question→passage) where it beats BM25 outright. A 1,500 docs/sec CPU encoder that needs no accelerator is a genuinely different operational story from a transformer.

Skip static when: relevance depends on word order or composition, the corpus is lexically dense enough that BM25 already wins (and BM25 is free), or you have the GPU budget and the task needs a real transformer to clear BM25 at all (fiqa was this case — MiniLM 0.36 vs static 0.20 vs BM25 0.23).

## Limitations

**Four datasets, all small.** arguana (8.7k), nfcorpus (3.6k), scifact (5.2k), fiqa (57.6k) — chosen to span lexical overlap and stay feasible for exact CPU search. The lexical-overlap pattern is robust across them, but the absolute numbers are dataset-specific; don't read the arguana win as "static beats BM25 everywhere."

**Use the retrieval-tuned model.** `potion-base-32M` is distilled on general C4 text and trails its retrieval fine-tune `potion-retrieval-32M` by ~13% NDCG. The base model is the wrong thing to benchmark for search — a mistake easy to make since it's the "default" potion.

**Order blindness is structural, not tunable.** No amount of dimension or training fixes permutation invariance — it's baked into mean-pooling a lookup table. Models that need word order can't be static.

**Vocabulary is frozen at training time.** A static table can't adapt to truly novel terms at query time; out-of-vocabulary tokens fall back to subword averages. potion uses its own vocabulary (512-d) while static-mrl reuses BERT's (1024-d) — compare them by quality and latency, not by dimension.

## Key Takeaways

- **Static embeddings drop the transformer entirely** — tokenize, look up one vector per token, average. The retrieval-tuned `potion-retrieval-32M` hits **97.9% of all-MiniLM's NDCG@10 on scifact (and beats its Recall@10) at ~90× lower query latency and ~80× faster indexing**, all on CPU.
- **Static dense beats BM25 exactly where lexical matching breaks down.** On arguana (argument→counter-argument, low word overlap) both static models clear BM25 (+5.6%); on lexically dense scifact/fiqa, BM25 wins. The verdict flips on the _task_, not the model.
- **Use the retrieval-tuned variant.** `potion-retrieval-32M` beats general-purpose `potion-base-32M` by ~13% NDCG — same table, fine-tuned on MS MARCO/QA data. Benchmarking the base model understates static retrieval badly.
- **Static embeddings are exactly word-order blind.** Any reordering of the same tokens yields a byte-identical vector (cosine 1.000). A transformer is only _approximately_ order-sensitive. The cost is composition, not vocabulary — where words _differ_, static ≈ transformer.
- **Matryoshka truncation to 256-d keeps 95% of quality** at a quarter of the storage; below 128-d it falls off a cliff (64-d → 77%, 32-d → 52%).
